# **Navigating Intellectual Property Ownership of AI-Generated Content in New Zealand Employment**

## **I. Executive Summary**

The proliferation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) in New Zealand workplaces presents novel and complex challenges to established principles of Intellectual Property (IP) ownership, particularly within the employer-employee relationship. This report examines the impact of AI-generated content on traditional IP ownership clauses in New Zealand employment contracts. While the Copyright Act 1994 and standard contractual provisions *prima facie* favour employer ownership of work created by employees in the course of their employment, the unique characteristics of AI-generated content—specifically concerning authorship, originality, and the nature of AI training data—introduce significant ambiguities and potential risks.

Under New Zealand law, the "author" of a computer-generated work is deemed to be the person by whom the arrangements necessary for its creation were undertaken. In an employment context, this is typically the employee interacting with the AI tool. If this "arrangement" and the resultant work meet the threshold for originality, requiring sufficient human skill, labour, and judgment, then copyright may subsist and, by default or contractual agreement, vest in the employer. However, the level of human input required for originality in AI-assisted works is a developing area of law, and outputs from minimal prompting may not attract copyright protection.

Furthermore, the terms of service of AI tools themselves can complicate IP ownership, with some providers claiming rights to outputs or the data inputted by users. Employers face critical risks, including potential copyright infringement stemming from AI outputs trained on unlicensed data, breaches of confidentiality if sensitive information is inputted into AI tools, and vicarious liability for employees' misuse of AI.

The current New Zealand regulatory landscape is "light-touch," relying on existing legislation which is, in some respects, dated concerning advanced AI. Consequently, employers cannot afford a passive approach. Proactive measures are essential, including reviewing and amending employment contract IP clauses to explicitly address AI-generated works, developing robust workplace AI usage policies that govern data handling and tool selection, and providing comprehensive employee training. Diligence in selecting AI vendors and understanding their IP terms is also paramount. Ultimately, balancing the innovative potential of AI with responsible IP management requires a strategic, informed, and adaptive approach from New Zealand employers.

## **II. Introduction: AI, Employment, and the Shifting IP Landscape in New Zealand**

The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools into New Zealand workplaces is rapidly transforming how content, code, and other intellectual outputs are created.1 From generating marketing copy and software code to assisting in research and design, AI's capabilities are being leveraged across a multitude of industries.3 This technological shift, while offering significant productivity gains, concurrently introduces profound questions for the established legal frameworks governing Intellectual Property (IP), particularly within the employment context. The rapid pace of AI advancement has, in many instances, outstripped the development of specific legal and contractual adaptations, leaving many New Zealand businesses navigating a landscape of uncertainty.5

The central inquiry of this report revolves around a critical issue: How do traditional IP ownership clauses, commonly found in New Zealand employment contracts, apply to works generated or significantly assisted by AI? These clauses typically stipulate that the employer retains ownership of any work-related IP created by an employee during their job duties. However, the nature of AI-generated content—often derived from vast datasets sourced from the internet and produced via complex algorithms—challenges conventional notions of human creativity, authorship, and originality.5 The "black box" nature of many AI models, where the precise lineage of training data and the mechanics of output generation are not always transparent, further complicates the application of traditional IP principles that rely on identifiable human contribution and clear provenance of works.5

This report aims to provide an expert analysis of this evolving intersection of AI, employment law, and IP rights in New Zealand. It will delve into the foundations of IP ownership under the Copyright Act 1994 and standard employment agreements. Subsequently, it will examine the specific legal considerations pertinent to AI-generated content, focusing on authorship and originality. The impact of these considerations on the enforceability and interpretation of standard employment IP clauses will be assessed, followed by an identification of critical risks and liabilities for employers. The report will also consider the current regulatory environment in New Zealand and draw on international perspectives. Finally, it will offer strategic recommendations for New Zealand employers in technology-centric and other content-creating industries to navigate this complex terrain, aiming to foster innovation while responsibly managing IP assets and mitigating legal risks. The insights provided are intended to assist businesses in understanding their rights and obligations in an era where AI is an increasingly integral part of the creative and developmental process.

## **III. Foundations of IP Ownership in New Zealand Employment**

Understanding the impact of AI-generated content on IP ownership within employment first requires an appreciation of the existing legal bedrock. In New Zealand, this foundation is primarily built upon the Copyright Act 1994 and the specific terms articulated in employment contracts.

### **A. The Copyright Act 1994: Default Ownership Rules**

Copyright in New Zealand is an automatic right that comes into existence when an original work of a specific type is created, published, or performed; no formal registration is required.7 The Copyright Act 1994 (the "Act") details the types of works to which this protection applies, including literary, dramatic, musical, and artistic works, as well as computer programs and films.9

A cornerstone provision for the employment context is Section 21(2) of the Act. This section stipulates: "Where a literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic work, or a film, is made by an employee in the course of his or her employment, his or her employer is the first owner of any copyright in the work subject to any agreement to the contrary." This establishes a clear default position: IP created by an employee as part of their job belongs to the employer, unless the employment agreement explicitly states otherwise.7 The phrase "in the course of employment" is generally interpreted based on the scope of the employee's duties and whether the creation of the work falls within that scope.

It is also important to note the existence of moral rights under the Act. These include the right of the author to be identified and the right to object to derogatory treatment of their work.8 Unlike economic rights (copyright ownership), moral rights generally remain with the human creator (the employee) even if the copyright in the work is owned by the employer. These rights cannot be assigned but can be waived.

The default employer ownership rule under Section 21(2) provides a strong initial basis for employers' claims to IP. However, its straightforward application to AI-generated or AI-assisted content becomes nuanced. The critical question is whether content significantly shaped by an AI tool can be considered a "work" that is "made by the employee" in the traditional sense anticipated by the Act. If the AI is merely a tool, akin to a word processor, then Section 21(2) applies more readily. Conversely, if the AI's contribution is so substantial that it overshadows the employee's input, the interpretation of "made by the employee" becomes less certain, potentially necessitating clearer contractual language to affirm employer ownership in such scenarios.

### **B. Standard Intellectual Property Clauses in Employment Contracts**

Complementing the statutory framework, employment contracts in New Zealand routinely include specific clauses addressing IP ownership. These clauses are designed to reinforce and often broaden the employer's rights beyond the default position in the Copyright Act 1994\. As outlined in the user query, such clauses typically assert that:

1. The employer retains ownership of any work-related intellectual property created by the employee while performing their job duties.  
2. Employees may be required to assign any rights they might have over such work to the company.  
3. The clause ensures that the company can use, modify, and profit from the work without needing further permission from the employee.

The New Zealand government's Employment Agreement Builder provides guidance that aligns with these principles, suggesting clauses that state: "Anything the employee invents, develops, creates or makes as part of their job or in the employer's time is the intellectual property of the employer. The employer must be told about it immediately and the employee agrees to take any necessary steps to transfer ownership".12 This covers various forms of IP, including trademarks, patents, designs, and copyright in original works like written material and drawings.12

The importance of clarity in these contractual IP clauses cannot be overstated, as ambiguity can lead to costly and time-consuming disputes with current or former employees.12 For instance, specific agreements are often recommended for situations like after-hours work where employees might use business equipment for personal projects, to delineate ownership clearly.12

Standard IP clauses are intentionally drafted with broad language, aiming to capture the full spectrum of intellectual property that an employee might generate. Terms like "invents, develops, creates or makes" are intended to be comprehensive. However, the advent of sophisticated AI tools introduces a new dynamic. When an employee utilizes an AI tool to produce content, a question arises as to whether the employee is truly "creating" or "making" the output in the traditional sense, or if their role is more akin to "arranging for the creation" of the work – a concept specifically relevant to computer-generated works under the Copyright Act 1994\. These standard clauses, often predating the widespread use of generative AI, may not have explicitly contemplated this distinction. Consequently, without specific reference to AI-assisted or AI-generated works, their application might be open to interpretative challenges, particularly if the employee's direct creative input is minimal compared to the AI's generative contribution. This potential ambiguity could weaken an employer's otherwise strong claim to the IP.

## **IV. AI-Generated Content and Copyright in New Zealand: Key Legal Considerations**

The interaction of AI with New Zealand's copyright framework brings forth specific legal considerations crucial for determining IP ownership, particularly concerning authorship and originality.

### **A. Authorship of Computer-Generated Works: Section 5(2)(a) of the Copyright Act 1994**

A pivotal provision in New Zealand's Copyright Act 1994 for AI-generated content is Section 5(2)(a). It defines the "author" of a work, stating that "in the case of a literary, dramatic, musical, or artistic work that is computer-generated, the person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken" is deemed the author.11 This is a significant departure from jurisdictions that strictly require a human author in a more direct, creative capacity, making New Zealand somewhat of an outlier in its approach to computer-generated works.5

The prevailing interpretation of Section 5(2)(a) in the context of generative AI is that the "person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken" is typically the individual who inputs the prompts, parameters, or instructions into the AI tool.11 This means that the user guiding the AI, rather than the AI itself or its original programmer, is considered the author for copyright purposes, provided the other conditions for copyright (like originality) are met.

This provision has profound implications in the employment context. If an employee, in the course of their employment, undertakes these "arrangements necessary" (e.g., by prompting an AI tool to generate code, text, or designs), that employee is deemed the author of the computer-generated work.11 Consequently, the default rule under Section 21(2) of the Act (employer ownership of employee-created works) or the explicit IP assignment clauses in their employment contract would then typically operate to transfer copyright ownership from the employee-author to the employer. Section 5(2)(a) thus provides a crucial link in the chain of IP ownership for employers in New Zealand. Without this provision, if the AI were considered the author, employers would struggle to claim ownership, as an AI cannot be an employee or assign rights.

However, the phrase "arrangements necessary for the creation of the work" is not exhaustively defined within the Act and remains subject to interpretation, especially as AI tools evolve in autonomy and complexity.14 Questions may arise regarding the threshold of human interaction required to constitute "arrangements." For instance, if an employee merely clicks a button to initiate a largely automated AI generation process with minimal specific instruction, it could be debated whether their involvement was substantial enough to be deemed the "arranger".15 This ambiguity underscores the importance of detailed record-keeping of employee inputs and interactions with AI tools 14 and suggests a need for employment contracts to clearly address expectations around AI use and the definition of "arrangements."

### **B. Originality of AI-Generated Content: The "Skill, Labour, and Judgment" Test**

Even if an employee is identified as the "author" of an AI-generated work under Section 5(2)(a), for copyright to subsist, the work itself must be "original." Originality in New Zealand copyright law does not mean that the idea behind the work must be novel or inventive; rather, the *expression* of that idea must originate from the author and not be copied from another source.9 Crucially, the creation of the work must involve the exercise of "independent skill and labour" by the author.9

The leading New Zealand authority on originality is the Court of Appeal case *University of Waikato v Benchmarking Services Ltd (2004) 8 NZBLC 101,561*. This case established that for a work to be original, "sufficient time, skill, labour, or judgement must have been expended in producing the work".11 The threshold for originality is generally considered not to be particularly high; the work does not need to be novel or possess artistic merit in the traditional sense, but it must be more than a trivial or slavish copy.11

Applying this "skill, labour, and judgment" test to AI-generated content raises complex questions. If an employee provides a simple, generic prompt to an AI tool and uses the output verbatim, it is questionable whether sufficient employee skill, labour, or judgment has been invested in that specific output to render it original from the perspective of the employee's contribution.11 In such cases, the creative effort might be seen as residing primarily with the AI model and its underlying programming, or potentially with the creators of the data on which the AI was trained.

Conversely, if an employee significantly edits, curates, refines, or creatively combines AI-generated outputs, or engages in a detailed iterative process of prompting and selection, their contribution of skill, labour, and judgment is more apparent.11 Such substantial human intervention would more likely satisfy the originality requirement, allowing copyright to subsist in the final human-modified work, or at least in the human-authored modifications and arrangement.

While not binding in New Zealand, the stance of the U.S. Copyright Office is instructive. It has indicated that mere prompts are generally insufficient to qualify the user as the author of AI-generated output for copyright purposes; more substantial and creative human control over the expressive elements of the work is required.17 This aligns with the principle that copyright protects human authorship.

The critical nuance for employers is that even if an employee is deemed the "author" by making "arrangements" under Section 5(2)(a), if the AI-generated work itself lacks originality due to trivial human input, there may be no copyright for the employer to own. The nature and extent of the employee's intellectual contribution are paramount. This uncertainty highlights a significant area of ongoing legal debate globally and in New Zealand regarding the precise level of "sufficient human contribution" or "skill, labour, and judgment" needed for AI-assisted works to be copyrightable.1 There is currently no definitive bright-line rule, making a case-by-case assessment necessary.

### **C. The Influence of AI Tool Terms of Service on IP Rights**

Beyond the Copyright Act 1994 and employment agreements, the Terms of Service (ToS) of the AI tools themselves introduce another layer of complexity to IP ownership.5 Many AI tool providers include clauses in their ToS that dictate ownership or grant extensive usage rights over both the data inputted by users (prompts, uploaded materials) and the outputs generated by the AI.5

These ToS can vary significantly. Some providers may assert ownership over the generated outputs, or claim broad, irrevocable licenses to use, modify, and distribute them.5 For instance, the terms for Stability AI have differed from those of OpenAI's ChatGPT, with the latter generally assigning rights in the output to the user, subject to compliance with their terms.20 Such provisions can directly conflict with an employer's IP claims derived through an employment contract. If an employee, in using an AI tool, agrees to ToS (often through a click-wrap agreement) that grant rights to the AI provider, this could supersede or complicate the employer's assertion of ownership under the employment agreement. This creates a scenario where the specific agreement related to the tool's use might prevail, or at least create significant legal ambiguity.

Employers must, therefore, diligently review and understand the ToS of any AI tools their employees are permitted to use.20 It is insufficient to rely on employees to navigate these often complex legal documents. Where possible, negotiating enterprise-level agreements with AI providers can allow businesses to secure more favorable IP and data usage terms that align with their own IP policies and employment contract provisions.

Recognizing the IP risks inherent in AI-generated content, some major AI platforms, such as Adobe and Google, have begun offering their users indemnities against third-party IP infringement lawsuits arising from the use of their AI tools.5 This is a significant market trend, reflecting an acknowledgement by providers of the legal anxieties surrounding AI outputs and likely serving as a competitive differentiator. While these indemnities are a welcome development, employers should scrutinize their scope and limitations carefully. They may not cover all scenarios or types of IP, and reliance on them should not replace robust internal IP management practices. Nevertheless, favoring tools that offer such protections can be a prudent part of an employer's risk mitigation strategy.

## **V. Impact of Standard Employment IP Clauses on AI-Generated Content**

The central question for employers is whether their existing, standard IP ownership clauses in employment contracts are robust enough to capture works created with significant AI assistance. While these clauses are typically broad, their application to AI-generated content is not always straightforward.

### **A. Do Employer Ownership Rights Automatically Extend to AI-Facilitated Creations?**

Standard IP clauses in New Zealand employment contracts, as previously discussed, generally assign IP created by an employee "in the course of employment" to the employer.12 The core issue is whether content that is substantially generated by an AI, but prompted, curated, or modified by an employee, is "created by the employee" in a manner that these clauses unambiguously capture.

The interplay between New Zealand's copyright law and these contractual clauses is key. If an employee is deemed the "author" of a computer-generated work under Section 5(2)(a) of the Copyright Act 1994 (by making the "arrangements necessary"), and if that work is "original" due to sufficient employee skill, labour, and judgment, then the IP clause in the employment contract *should* operate to transfer ownership of that copyright to the employer.11 In such instances, the AI is effectively treated as a sophisticated tool used by the employee to create the work.

However, the strength of the employer's claim under a standard clause weakens in scenarios where:

1. **Minimal Human Input:** If the employee's contribution is trivial (e.g., a very basic prompt leading to a complex AI output) and the resulting work arguably lacks originality stemming from the employee's efforts, there may be no new copyright for the employer to claim. The phrase "work created by employee" is tested here; if the AI is seen as the primary "creator" and the employee's input is negligible, the contractual nexus may be challenged.  
2. **Conflicting AI Tool ToS:** If the terms of service of the AI tool used by the employee grant ownership or extensive rights to the AI provider, this can directly conflict with and potentially override the employer's IP clause, as discussed previously.

Standard IP clauses are likely effective for AI-assisted work where the employee's creative contribution is clearly dominant and meets the originality threshold. For example, if an employee uses AI to generate a rough draft and then extensively rewrites, edits, and adds substantial original material, the final work is more clearly "created by the employee." Conversely, where the AI's generative contribution is overwhelming and the employee's input is limited to simple instructions, the automatic extension of employer ownership via standard clauses becomes less certain. These clauses were generally drafted without the current capabilities of generative AI in mind, and their language may not adequately address the nuances of human-AI collaboration in content creation.

### **B. Analyzing Ambiguities and Potential for Disputes**

The ambiguities inherent in applying pre-AI era IP clauses to AI-generated content create a fertile ground for potential disputes between employers and employees (or former employees).6 If a contract is not explicit about how AI-assisted works are treated, parties may develop differing good-faith interpretations of their rights, especially if the AI-generated output proves to be valuable.

Disputes could arise if an employee argues that their use of an AI tool, and the resultant output, falls outside the traditional scope of "their" creation as envisioned by the employment contract. They might contend that the AI, rather than their own "skill, labour, and judgment," was the primary source of the valuable elements of the work, thereby challenging the basis for automatic assignment of IP to the employer. This is particularly relevant if the employee's role was more akin to that of a curator or prompter rather than a traditional author or inventor.

The increasing use of personal AI accounts or AI tools not officially sanctioned by the employer further complicates matters.24 If an employee uses a personal AI subscription, the ToS of that tool (which the employer may not be privy to) will apply. If they create something valuable using such a tool, even if related to their work, ownership could become highly contested, especially if the work was done partially outside of work hours or using personal equipment, blurring the lines of "in the course of employment."

The cost and time involved in defending IP ownership disputes can be substantial.12 Therefore, the lack of clarity in existing contractual language regarding AI-generated content is not merely a theoretical concern but a tangible risk that can lead to significant legal and financial repercussions for employers. Proactive clarification through updated employment contracts and comprehensive workplace policies is a critical strategy for mitigating these dispute risks.

## **VI. Critical Risks and Liabilities for Employers**

The integration of AI into workplace content creation processes, while offering benefits, also exposes employers to a range of critical risks and potential liabilities. These extend beyond ownership disputes to include infringement, confidentiality breaches, and vicarious liability.

### **A. Infringement Risks from AI Training Data and Outputs**

A primary concern is the risk of copyright infringement stemming from both the data used to train AI models and the outputs these models generate. Generative AI systems are trained on colossal datasets, frequently "scraped" from the internet without explicit permission from copyright holders.5 This training data inevitably includes vast amounts of copyrighted material, such as text, images, music, and code.5

Consequently, AI-generated outputs may inadvertently reproduce or be substantially similar to existing copyrighted works, leading to infringement claims against the user of the AI tool.5 If an employee uses an AI tool to generate content for business purposes, and that content infringes a third party's copyright (e.g., because the AI copied protected elements from its training data), the employer, as the entity using and benefiting from the infringing material, can be held directly liable for copyright infringement.20 This risk is underscored by ongoing high-profile global lawsuits, such as those involving Getty Images and The New York Times, against AI developers over the use of copyrighted material for training AI models.5

The legal landscape in New Zealand may present additional challenges. New Zealand's Copyright Act 1994 provides for "fair dealing" exceptions, which are more restricted in scope than the "fair use" doctrine found in the United States.20 This narrower scope of exceptions could mean that the act of training AI models on copyrighted New Zealand works is less likely to be excused as fair dealing, potentially increasing the risk of underlying infringement by the AI tools themselves, which then flows through to the users of those tools. Employers must therefore exercise caution, as reliance on AI outputs without due diligence can lead to significant legal and financial consequences.

### **B. Confidentiality, Data Security, and Privacy Concerns with AI Tool Usage**

The use of AI tools by employees, particularly public or third-party external AI platforms, raises serious concerns regarding confidentiality, data security, and privacy.5 When employees input confidential company information (e.g., trade secrets, strategic plans, unpublished financial data), client data, or personal information into these AI tools, there is a substantial risk of unauthorized disclosure, data breaches, or loss of proprietary value.24

Many AI tool providers may store, process, and even use the data inputted by users, potentially for the purpose of further training their AI models, unless users explicitly opt out or the ToS prohibit such use.20 This means that sensitive information, once entered into an AI prompt, could become part of the AI's knowledge base or be accessible to the AI provider, undermining its confidentiality.24 For example, inputting proprietary source code into an AI tool for assistance could lead to that code no longer being a trade secret if the AI provider's terms permit its use.

Reflecting these concerns, both the New Zealand Law Society and New Zealand government guidance strongly advise exercising extreme caution when inputting personal or client information into external AI tools.28 The potential for misuse or unintended exposure of such data can lead to breaches of the Privacy Act 2020, contractual confidentiality obligations to clients, and significant reputational damage. Uncontrolled employee use of AI tools for tasks involving sensitive data thus represents a critical operational and compliance risk that can have severe repercussions, irrespective of IP ownership in the AI-generated output.

### **C. Employer (Vicarious) Liability for Employee Actions**

Under established legal principles in New Zealand, employers can be held vicariously liable for wrongful acts committed by their employees if those acts occur in the course of their employment.33 This principle extends to the use of AI tools. If an employee, while performing their job duties, uses an AI tool in a way that results in copyright infringement, defamation (e.g., AI generating false and harmful statements), or other legal wrongs, the employer may be held liable alongside the employee.26

This liability can arise even if the employer was not directly aware of the specific infringing use of AI, provided the employee's actions were within the general scope of their employment responsibilities.26 Key factors in determining vicarious liability often include the employer's right and ability to supervise the infringing activity and whether the employer had a direct financial interest in such activities.26 Given that AI use is often intended to benefit the employer's business, these conditions may frequently be met.

The lack of clear, enforced policies and adequate training regarding the permissible and impermissible uses of AI in the workplace significantly heightens the risk of vicarious liability. If an employer has not taken reasonable steps to guide employee conduct concerning AI tools, it becomes more difficult to argue that an employee's misuse was outside the scope of their employment or unforeseeable. Therefore, implementing and diligently enforcing AI usage policies is not just a matter of good governance but a crucial step in mitigating potential vicarious liability for harm caused by AI-assisted employee actions.

## **VII. The Evolving Legal and Regulatory Environment**

The legal and regulatory landscape governing AI, particularly concerning IP, is in a state of flux globally and in New Zealand. Employers must navigate this evolving environment with an awareness of current domestic laws, the stance of regulatory bodies, and influential international trends.

### **A. Current Stance of New Zealand Law and IPONZ**

New Zealand's primary legislative instrument for copyright, the Copyright Act 1994, does contain a provision, Section 5(2)(a), that allows for copyright to subsist in computer-generated works, attributing authorship to the "person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken".11 This provision provides a potential pathway for human authorship (and subsequent employer ownership) of AI-generated content, a stance that is more accommodating than in some jurisdictions that demand more direct human creative input.14

The Intellectual Property Office of New Zealand (IPONZ) is responsible for the registration of certain IP rights (like patents and trademarks) and provides public information on IP matters.7 However, copyright arises automatically and does not require registration with IPONZ. As of early May 2025, IPONZ's publicly available guidance does not offer specific, detailed directives on the nuances of IP ownership for AI-generated content within an employment context.34 Its role is more administrative and informational regarding existing law rather than interpretive in complex, emerging areas like AI.

The New Zealand government has generally adopted a "light-touch, proportionate, and risk-based" regulatory approach towards AI.1 The preference is to adapt existing legal frameworks where possible, rather than enacting comprehensive, standalone AI-specific legislation.36 The Ministry of Business, Innovation and Employment (MBIE) is actively involved in developing an AI strategy for New Zealand and producing responsible AI guidance for businesses and the public sector, aiming to align with OECD AI Principles.37 This work is ongoing, and while it signals governmental attention to AI, it has not yet resulted in major legislative amendments specifically targeting IP issues in AI-generated content.

There is a sentiment among some legal commentators that the Copyright Act 1994, drafted well before the advent of sophisticated generative AI, is becoming outdated and may not adequately address the complexities posed by modern technology.6 An issues paper for a review of the Act was released by MBIE in 2018 40, but significant reforms directly addressing AI and copyright in employment are yet to be legislated. This means that, for now, businesses must operate by interpreting existing laws, a task made more challenging by the lack of specific AI regulations or definitive case law directly on point.

### **B. Calls for Legislative Reform and International Perspectives**

The rapid advancements in generative AI have led to calls from some quarters for legislative reform of the Copyright Act 1994 to provide greater clarity and address the unique challenges posed by AI.6 One concern raised is whether granting copyright protection to AI-generated art or other content truly serves the underlying purpose of copyright law, which is often seen as incentivizing human creativity.6

Internationally, approaches to AI and copyright vary. While New Zealand, the UK, India, and Hong Kong have provisions that contemplate copyright in computer-generated works 16, many other countries adhere more strictly to a requirement for direct human authorship for copyright to subsist.1 This divergence means that the copyright status of AI-generated content can differ across borders, an important consideration for businesses operating internationally.

Global lawsuits concerning the use of copyrighted materials for training AI models are significantly shaping the international legal discourse and are being closely watched.5 The outcomes of these cases, particularly in influential jurisdictions like the US and UK, could have ripple effects on how AI training data is treated worldwide.

The U.S. Copyright Office has issued guidance (which, while not binding on New Zealand courts, is influential in international IP circles) emphasizing that "sufficient human control" and creative contribution are necessary for an AI-assisted work to be copyrightable by a human.17 Simply providing prompts to an AI system is generally not considered sufficient for the user to claim authorship of the AI's output.18 This focus on the quantum and nature of human creative input is a recurring theme in international discussions.

In New Zealand, the New Zealand Law Society | Te Kāhui Ture o Aotearoa has recognized the challenges AI poses for the legal profession and has launched a research project in 2025 to understand how lawyers are using AI, the risks they face, and the support they need.45 This initiative, ongoing as of April-May 2025, aims to inform future guidance and educational programs.46 While this research is focused on the legal profession, its findings and any subsequent guidance will likely contribute to a broader understanding and better practices regarding AI and IP within New Zealand.

The "light-touch" regulatory approach in New Zealand means that businesses must currently navigate these complexities by applying existing legal principles. However, the dynamic international environment, coupled with domestic calls for clarity and ongoing research by professional bodies, suggests that New Zealand's approach to AI and IP may evolve. Businesses should therefore remain vigilant, monitoring both domestic discussions and significant international legal developments, as these may foreshadow future refinements in New Zealand law and practice.

## **VIII. Strategic Recommendations for New Zealand Employers**

Given the current legal uncertainties and potential risks associated with AI-generated content, New Zealand employers must adopt a proactive and strategic approach to managing IP within the employment relationship. This involves a multi-faceted strategy encompassing contractual amendments, robust policy development, and ongoing operational diligence.

### **B. Developing Robust Workplace AI Usage Policies: Content, Code, and Data Handling**

Alongside contractual amendments, the development and implementation of clear, comprehensive, and consistently enforced workplace AI usage policies are crucial.25 These policies provide day-to-day operational guidance for employees and serve as a key risk mitigation tool. Such policies should, at a minimum, address:

* **Approved AI Tools:** Maintain a list of AI tools that have been vetted and approved for workplace use. Prohibit the use of unapproved or personal AI tools for company work, particularly those that have not undergone a security and IP terms review.24  
* **Data Input Guidelines:** Establish strict rules regarding the type of information that can be inputted into AI tools. Critically, this should include prohibitions or stringent approval processes for inputting confidential company information, trade secrets, client data, personal information, or any sensitive material into external or public AI platforms.28 Guidance should emphasize anonymization or the use of synthetic data where feasible.  
* **Human Review and Verification:** Mandate human oversight and verification of all AI-generated content before it is used for business purposes, especially if it is to be used externally or for critical decision-making. This review should check for accuracy, factual correctness, potential bias, and any signs of IP infringement (e.g., by using plagiarism checkers or reverse image searches).25  
* **Attribution and Disclosure:** Define circumstances under which the use of AI in creating content must be disclosed, both internally and externally. This is important for transparency and managing expectations.48  
* **IP Ownership and Reporting:** Reinforce the company's IP ownership position as stated in employment contracts. Establish clear procedures for employees to report the creation of potentially valuable IP using AI tools.  
* **Approval for New Tools/Uses:** Outline a process for employees to request approval for using new AI tools not on the approved list, or for using approved tools for novel applications. This allows for proper vetting before widespread adoption.  
* **Security Protocols:** Integrate AI usage guidelines with existing data security and cybersecurity policies to ensure a cohesive approach to protecting information assets.  
* **Consequences of Non-Compliance:** Clearly state the consequences for breaching the AI usage policy, which may include disciplinary action.

Well-communicated and consistently enforced AI policies are vital. They not only guide employee behavior and reduce the likelihood of IP-related incidents but also demonstrate the employer's commitment to responsible AI governance, which can be crucial in mitigating vicarious liability.25

### **C. Employee Training, Due Diligence on AI Tools, and Vendor Agreements**

Contractual clauses and written policies form the legal and procedural backbone, but their effectiveness hinges on practical, operational measures. These include:

* **Comprehensive Employee Training:** Regular and ongoing training is essential to ensure employees understand the company's AI policies, the IP risks involved (including infringement and confidentiality), data security protocols, and ethical considerations when using AI tools.24 Training should cover practical aspects like identifying potentially infringing outputs and understanding the limitations of AI.  
* **Due Diligence on AI Tools and Vendors:** Before approving any AI tool for workplace use, employers must conduct thorough due diligence.21 This involves carefully scrutinizing the vendor's ToS, particularly clauses related to IP ownership of inputs and outputs, data usage rights (especially concerning model training), data security measures, and confidentiality undertakings.20 The availability and scope of any IP infringement indemnities offered by the vendor should also be a key consideration.5  
* **Negotiating Vendor Agreements:** For critical AI tools or widespread deployment, employers should seek to negotiate enterprise-level agreements with AI vendors. This provides an opportunity to secure more favorable terms regarding IP ownership, data confidentiality, usage rights, liability, and indemnification, rather than relying on standard click-through ToS.1  
* **Documenting Creative Input:** Encourage and train employees to maintain records of their creative process when using AI tools. This includes documenting significant prompts, iterations, selection rationale for AI outputs, and the nature and extent of their subsequent modifications, editing, or integration work.14 Such documentation can be invaluable in supporting claims of originality and human authorship should IP disputes arise.

A holistic approach that combines robust legal frameworks (contracts and policies) with practical operational safeguards (training, due diligence, documentation) is necessary for New Zealand employers to effectively manage IP in an environment increasingly shaped by AI-driven creation. This integrated strategy will best position businesses to leverage AI's benefits while mitigating the associated legal and financial risks.

## **IX. Conclusion: Proactive Management in an Age of AI-Driven Creation**

The increasing use of AI for content generation in New Zealand workplaces introduces considerable complexity to the established norms of intellectual property ownership within employment. While New Zealand's Copyright Act 1994, through its unique provision for authorship of computer-generated works by the "arranger," offers a potential pathway for employers to claim IP developed by employees using AI, this is far from a straightforward assertion.11 The requirements for originality, demanding sufficient employee "skill, labour, and judgment," mean that not all AI-assisted outputs will automatically qualify for copyright protection, particularly where human input is minimal.11

Standard IP clauses in existing employment contracts, drafted before the widespread advent of sophisticated generative AI, may not adequately address these nuances and are susceptible to ambiguity and potential disputes.12 Furthermore, the terms of service of AI tools themselves, coupled with risks of copyright infringement from AI training data and outputs, and breaches of confidentiality, present significant ongoing challenges for employers.5

Given New Zealand's current "light-touch" regulatory approach to AI, which relies on interpreting existing, sometimes dated, legislation 1, the onus falls squarely on employers to be proactive. Waiting for definitive legislative reform or comprehensive judicial precedent is not a viable strategy in the face of rapidly evolving technology and its swift adoption in the workplace.6

Therefore, New Zealand employers must actively engage in reviewing and amending their employment contracts to explicitly address AI-generated and AI-assisted intellectual property. Concurrently, the development and diligent enforcement of robust, clear workplace AI usage policies are paramount. These policies should provide practical guidance on approved tools, data handling protocols, human verification of outputs, and IP reporting. Complementing these legal and policy measures with comprehensive employee training, thorough due diligence on AI vendors and their terms, and fostering a culture of responsible AI use will create a more resilient framework for IP management.

Ultimately, navigating the IP implications of AI-driven creation requires a dynamic and informed approach. By proactively adapting their legal agreements, internal policies, and operational practices, New Zealand employers can better position themselves to harness the transformative potential of AI while safeguarding their intellectual property assets, minimizing legal risks, and fostering an environment of responsible innovation.
